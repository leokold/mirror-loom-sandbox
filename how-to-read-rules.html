How to read these rules

A two-minute primer on the cellular automata behind the sandbox.

← Back to the sandbox

Down the page is forward in time

A row of squares is the world at one instant. White is 0, black is 1. The top row is the starting state, and each row beneath it is the world one tick later, so reading down the page is watching time run. The diagrams in the sandbox are exactly this, just smaller, with hundreds of rows stacked.

Each cell is copied from three cells above it

To fill in a cell, look at the row directly above and take three cells: the one up and to the left, the one straight up, and the one up and to the right. Each is 0 or 1, so the three together form one of eight little patterns. A rule is nothing more than a decision, for each of those eight patterns, whether the cell below becomes black or white.

Three cells decide one cell. The rule supplies the answer for every possible trio.

The rule number is just that table

Three cells give eight patterns, running from 111 down to 000. Write down what each one turns into and you get eight 0s and 1s. Read those eight as a binary number and you have the rule's name, somewhere from 0 to 255. That is Stephen Wolfram's numbering. Here is Rule 90, whose answer is simply "black when exactly one neighbor is black," ignoring the middle cell.

Each trio (top) maps to one output (bottom). Reading the eight outputs from 111 to 000 gives the binary number 90.

The four rules, first rows

Same starting state for all four: a single black cell in the middle of a white row. Watch how differently they grow. The teal column is the cell the observer watches in the first experiment, read straight down as a stream of 0s and 1s.

Rule 250

order · left OR right

The simplest case. From one seed it grows a plain, regular triangle that repeats the same motif forever. Whatever you have seen, you already know what comes next.

Rule 90

fractal · left XOR right

A cell turns black when exactly one neighbor is black. That single rule draws the Sierpinski triangle, the same shape nested inside itself at every scale.

Rule 110

complex · universal

Structured shapes that drift sideways and collide. The interactions are rich enough to carry out any computation, which makes this rule a universal computer.

Rule 30

chaos · random generator

From one black cell it boils into static. One side settles into stripes while the other churns. Wolfram used this rule's center column as a random-number generator.

From a single seed the action spreads out from the middle, so the edges stay white at first and some center columns are quiet. The sandbox runs each rule from a full random row instead, which fills the screen and makes the watched column busier.

What the observer reads

The grid of cells, evolving by the rule, is the world. The observer in the sandbox is not one of those cells. It sits outside and reads only a thin slice: in the first experiment the teal center column, top to bottom, as a stream of 0s and 1s; the later experiments change what it looks at, the two neighboring cells, or a coarse, blocky view. All four experiments ask the same thing: how much of the world can that limited view reconstruct.
