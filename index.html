The bounded observer

A sandbox you can play with, and a plain walkthrough of what it shows.

The one idea

No mind sees all of reality. Your eyes take in a sliver of the light in a room, your brain throws most of it away, and what is left is a compressed, useful sketch you act on. Any observer that is part of the world, whether a cell, a brain, a computer, or an AI, faces the same problem: there is far more going on than it can track, so it must compress.

This page lets you measure that. It builds a tiny, fully known toy world, drops a deliberately limited observer inside it, and asks three questions: how much can the observer predict, does changing its viewpoint change what is knowable, and if you run it forward where does the arrow of time come from? The surprise is that the answers are not about the world alone. They are about the fit between the world and the observer looking at it.

The toy world: cellular automata

We need a world simple enough to understand completely yet rich enough to be interesting. A cellular automaton is perfect. Picture a long row of cells, each black (1) or white (0). Time moves in steps; at each step every cell updates from itself and its two neighbors by a fixed rule. There are 256 such rules, numbered 0 to 255 (the numbering is Stephen Wolfram's). Four of them cover the range: Rule 250 is pure order, Rule 90 draws the Sierpiński fractal, Rule 110 is complex enough to run any computation, and Rule 30 looks like static and was used as a random-number generator.

The whole point: the observer never sees the entire grid. It peeks through a keyhole. In the first two experiments it watches a single column of cells over time; in the third it sees only a blurry, blocky version. The rule you pick below drives all three experiments.

The measuring stick: bits

To grade prediction we need a number for "how surprised were you." Information theory gives one: the bit. Guessing a fair coin, your average surprise is 1 bit, the most a yes-or-no can deliver. A loaded coin surprises you less, so fewer bits; a sure thing, zero. So on our scale 1 bit per symbol means no grip at all, a coin flip, and 0 bits means perfect prediction. This average surprise is Claude Shannon's entropy, the basis of information theory since 1948, and everything in the three charts below is built from it.

Experiment 1: predict it

In plain terms: watch one cell, the highlighted column, and guess its next value from only that cell's own recent past, never from the rule itself. The guess is learned by tallying what usually came next. In detail: the observer uses the previous k values, where k is how much memory we allow. It learns on the first half of the run by counting, for each pattern of k past symbols, how often a 0 or a 1 came next; turns each tally into a probability p = (count + 1) / (total + 2) (the +1 is a humility rule so a pattern seen once is not over-trusted); and is graded on fresh held-out data by the surprise -log2(p) of the true next symbol, averaged into bits per symbol. Order is trivially predicted (0 bits), chaos sits at a 1-bit wall no matter how much memory you give it, and the complex rule drops toward 0 once memory is large enough, then flattens. That flat level is the floor, the best this observer can do. For chaos the floor is a wall: the only way to know the next symbol is to run the whole world, with no shortcut, which is what Wolfram called computational irreducibility. Experiment 2: swap the observer

Now hold the world completely fixed and change only the observer. The same rule keeps running and the cells do exactly what they did before. Three observers each try to predict one cell's next value, and the knob along the bottom is how many steps of that cell's own past they may use. The historian uses only that past. The neighbor observer ignores the past and looks sideways instead, at whether the cell's two immediate neighbors currently differ, their XOR; carrying no memory, its line stays flat. The third uses both, its own past and that one sideways glance.

Two things to keep straight. The memory knob moves only the historian and the memory half of the combined observer; it never widens the neighbor's view. And the neighbor's view is deliberately just the one-bit XOR of the two adjacent cells, not the full local patch. That restriction is the point: a cell's next value is computed from its three-cell neighborhood, so an observer allowed to see all three would predict any rule perfectly and there would be nothing to compare. Holding the neighbor to a lossy summary is what makes the contest meaningful.

Pick Rule 90 and the point lands hard. To the historian the cell is a coin flip, a full bit of surprise at every memory depth, apparently random. To the neighbor observer the very same stream is perfectly predictable, zero bits, because Rule 90's whole law is "a cell becomes the XOR of its neighbors." No amount of memory rescues the historian; the right instrument needs almost none. One calls it noise, the other calls it trivial, and both are right about the world they can see. The other rules fill in the lesson. Order (250) is free for everyone. The complex rule (110) rewards memory: the historian and the combined observer both descend as their context grows, while the memoryless neighbor view stalls. And chaos (30) is the wall: the historian never leaves the coin-flip line, and although the neighbor and combined observers do better they bottom out near a third of a bit and stay there. That residue is not coin noise. About half its steps are perfectly determined and the rest are educated guesses, right roughly three times in four; it is an irreducible remainder for this observer, the kind only full simulation from the true starting state could remove. For a cognitive scientist this is the load-bearing claim: a bee tuned to ultraviolet, an ear pitched elsewhere, a working memory of four chunks instead of seven, each is a different instrument carving a different knowable world from one shared reality.

Experiment 3: watch time appear

Start the world ordered, a small solid block, and let the observer see only a coarse, blocky version: lump the row into blocks of b cells and look at each block's pattern. Measure how scrambled that blurry view looks, step by step, with entropy again, computed as H = -(1/b) * sum over patterns v of f(v) * log2 f(v) in bits per cell. From the ordered start the blur's entropy climbs, fastest for chaos and barely at all for order. The punchline: the rule is completely deterministic, nothing random is added. The growing disorder is not in the world's steps, it is in the observer's blurry view failing to keep up. The arrow of time, order giving way to disorder, is something a coarse-graining observer manufactures. This is the second law of thermodynamics retold as a fact about observers, which is Wolfram's argument for why we feel time the way we do.

Experiment 4: the arrow runs both ways

Experiment 3 had a flaw worth fixing: those rules quietly lose a little information at the micro level, so part of the rising disorder was real, not only the observer's blur. Here we close that gap with a reversible world. The same rule is run in a form that destroys nothing, the next state is the rule applied to the present, flipped by the previous state, a recipe you can run backward exactly as well as forward. We start it at a single ordered moment and let it evolve both ways in time at once. The coarse entropy, the blurriness the observer sees, climbs as you move away from that moment, and it climbs the same whether you go forward or backward. The microscopic law has no preferred direction and loses no information; the arrow of time is entirely the observer's, manufactured from a low-entropy starting point seen through a coarse lens. Handed only the blurry view, an observer could not tell you which way the clock is running.

What it adds up to

One bounded observer, three faces of a single limit. Prediction runs into an irreducible floor, so some of the world cannot be compressed. Where that floor sits depends on the observer's grain, so what is knowable lives in the fit between world and observer, not in either alone. And running the observer forward, the arrow of time falls out of its coarse-graining, so completely that even a reversible world with no arrow of its own grows one for the observer. The bridge to cognitive science is direct: perception and thought are compression, the brain is a bounded observer making useful, lossy models of a world it can never fully hold, and what you can know, even your felt sense of time passing, is shaped by your limits as much as by what is out there.

Is any of this new math?

No, and that matters for trusting it. The tools are standard: Shannon's information theory (1948), simple memory-k prediction (n-gram models), Laplace smoothing, and block entropy. The deeper quantities the curves approach, the entropy rate and the predictable information, are studied formally in a field called computational mechanics. The only original move here is the framing, treating these three textbook measurements as one story about an observer. To go further you would reconstruct the system's smallest predictive model, an epsilon-machine, and use bias-corrected entropy estimates, which would sharpen the numbers without changing the shapes.

Honest limits

It is a toy: a cellular automaton is not the universe, so this shows a mechanism cleanly, it does not prove a worldview. The estimators are simple and slightly biased on small data, which is the little wiggle on the chaotic rule at high memory. These rules also lose some information at the micro level, so part of the rising disorder in experiment 3 is genuine, not purely the observer's blur; experiment 4 closes that gap with a reversible rule whose only source of rising disorder is the coarse-graining. And it is a classical, local model on purpose. There is a different kind of unpredictability in quantum physics, captured by Bell's theorem, that no model like this can reproduce. The wall this sandbox measures is the no-shortcut wall, not the quantum one. One honest caveat: that separation assumes measurement independence, meaning the experimenter's choices are not secretly correlated with the system. Superdeterministic readings of quantum mechanics deny exactly that, and on them the two walls stop being cleanly separate. No experiment can close that loophole, so the separation is the standard view rather than a settled fact.

Mini-glossary

Bit a unit of information or surprise, one yes-or-no's worth.
Entropy average surprise; high means unpredictable, low means predictable.
Bits per symbol the prediction error used here; 1 is a coin flip, 0 is perfect.
Accuracy the percentage of next-symbol guesses that are correct.
Markov / order-k model a predictor that uses the last k symbols.
Cross-entropy / log-loss the held-out average surprise, the main score.
Laplace smoothing adding 1 to each count so rare patterns are not over-trusted.
Coarse-graining looking at a blurred, summarized version instead of every detail.
Computational irreducibility when the only way to know the outcome is to run the whole process, no shortcut.
Entropy rate the unavoidable surprise per step that remains even with unlimited memory; the floor.
